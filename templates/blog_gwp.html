Global Warming Potential (GWP): A Thermodynamic & Regulatory Analysis

Updated: Dec 2025 Engineering Team Thermodynamics

For HVAC&R engineers, Global Warming Potential (GWP) is no longer just an environmental metricâ€”it is a primary design constraint. This article explores the physics behind GWP, its calculation methods, and the trade-offs between low-GWP fluids and thermodynamic performance.

1. The Physics of GWP: Radiative Forcing

Global Warming Potential is a relative measure of how much heat a greenhouse gas traps in the atmosphere. It compares the amount of heat trapped by a certain mass of the gas in question to the amount of heat trapped by a similar mass of carbon dioxide ($CO_2$).

Engineering Definition: GWP is defined as the ratio of the time-integrated radiative forcing from the instantaneous release of 1 kg of a trace substance relative to that of 1 kg of a reference gas ($CO_2$).

Mathematically, it is expressed as:

$$ GWP(x) = \frac{\int_{0}^{TH} a_x \cdot [x(t)] dt}{\int_{0}^{TH} a_{CO_2} \cdot [CO_2(t)] dt} $$

Where:

  • $TH$ is the time horizon (typically 20 or 100 years).
  • $a_x$ is the radiative efficiency of the substance (W m$^{-2}$ kg$^{-1}$).
  • $[x(t)]$ is the time-dependent decay of the abundance of the substance.

3. Regulatory Impact on System Design

Regulations such as the EU F-Gas Regulation (517/2014 & 2024 revision) and the EPA AIM Act mandate a phasedown of HFCs. This impacts compressor selection, heat exchanger sizing, and safety protocols.

  • Flammability (A2L/A3): Moving to low-GWP fluids often means dealing with flammability. Standards like IEC 60335-2-89 dictate charge limits.
  • Glide: Many low-GWP replacements are zeotropic blends (e.g., R448A, R449A) with significant temperature glide, affecting evaporator design.
  • Operating Pressures: R744 systems operate at pressures >100 bar, requiring specialized piping and safety valves.

4. Conclusion

Selecting a refrigerant is an optimization problem involving GWP, capacity, efficiency (COP), safety, and cost. Engineers must look beyond the GWP number and consider the Total Equivalent Warming Impact (TEWI), which accounts for both direct emissions (leaks) and indirect emissions (energy consumption).
